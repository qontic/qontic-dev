1. Begin with the classical prediction

A classical particle with total energy E below a barrier of height V0 cannot enter the barrier. Quantum mechanics changes that prediction because the state is a wavefunction, not just a point in phase space.

Throughout this notebook, ℏ = m = 1. The central momentum is k0, so the incident central energy is E0 = k02/2. The rendered state is a normalizable Gaussian packet, not a literal infinite plane wave.

Orange: square potential V(x). Blue: incident central energy E₀. When E₀<V₀, the classical prediction is R=1 and T=0. The rest of this notebook shows why the quantum calculation differs.
E₀ = k₀²/2
V(x) = V₀ for |x| ≤ a/2, and 0 otherwise
The barrier has finite width a, so the quantum wavefunction can extend across it.

2. The Schrödinger equation evolves a complex packet

The initial state is a Gaussian packet moving to the right. The visualization solves the one-dimensional time-dependent Schrödinger equation numerically with the same square barrier shown above.

Re ψ, Im ψ, and ρ(x,t)=|ψ|² are overlaid in one shared spatial plot. Their vertical display scales are fixed for the entire run, so spreading and attenuation remain visible. The cyan/blue wavefunction curves and the gold density curve use different fixed display normalizations, so compare their shapes and evolution rather than their plotted heights. Re ψ and Im ψ are useful components of a complex state, but neither is separately observable: a global phase rotation changes them while leaving ρ, j, and all detector probabilities unchanged.

What calculation is actually being performed?

The notebook evolves ψ with a finite-difference Crank–Nicolson step. For the closed finite grid used here, this is norm-conserving up to numerical roundoff:

i ∂ψ/∂t = [−½∂²/∂x² + V(x)]ψ
(I + iΔtH/2)ψⁿ⁺¹ = (I − iΔtH/2)ψⁿ

The numerical detector estimate is taken from a boundary-safe, separated snapshot: the readout reports both residual barrier probability and total norm. Part 4 compares the numerical packet with both T(E₀) and the momentum-averaged packet prediction.

3. Below the barrier, the local wave is evanescent

For a stationary component with E<V₀, the wave number inside the barrier becomes imaginary: k→iκ. The resulting spatial dependence is exponential rather than oscillatory.

This does not mean the packet simply vanishes at the first edge. A finite barrier supports a nonzero amplitude at its far edge. That surviving amplitude is the source of the transmitted packet.

The time control is placed near the collision. The numerical Re ψ, Im ψ, and ρ=|ψ|² remain combined in one plot. The dashed orange line is only a local stationary-state density guide exp[−2κ(x+a/2)].
κ = √[2(V₀−E₀)]   for E₀<V₀
ψII(x) = A eκx + B e−κx
A single decaying exponential alone carries no probability current. The physical barrier solution contains both exponentials with complex coefficients fixed by matching at both edges.

4. Transmission is controlled by barrier width and energy

For a monochromatic incident wave, a finite square barrier has an exact transmission coefficient. Below the barrier it is exponentially small for a thick or high barrier, but not generically zero.

The green curve is the exact stationary-plane-wave result. A finite Gaussian packet contains a range of wave numbers, so its appropriate comparison is the cyan momentum average ∫|ψ̃(k)|²T(k²/2)dk, not only the gold single-energy value T(E₀). A narrower momentum distribution makes the two benchmarks converge.

5. One detection at a time; a frequency after many runs

Do not read the final split packet as many particles in one experiment. First prepare one identical packet, then record one localized detector position. Repeating that preparation builds a frequency record.

Reading the canvas from top to bottom: (1) the gold late-time density specifies the probability of a detector position; (2) every red or green dot is one independent experimental run sampled from that density; (3) the lower bars compare the observed fractions with the quantum prediction. A dot is not a particle trajectory and the collection of dots is not one simultaneous multi-particle snapshot.

6. The wavefunction prediction is shared; the ontological story changes

Both choices use the same Schrödinger equation, barrier potential, density, current, and detector statistics. They disagree about whether an unobserved particle has a definite configuration throughout the experiment.

Interpretation note

Orthodox interpretation

ψ is a probability amplitude. It evolves unitarily before measurement; a detector event is localized and has probabilities given by the Born rule. This presentation does not add a definite, measurement-independent particle path to the state description. Re ψ and Im ψ are display components whose split depends on a global phase convention.

Pilot-wave / Bohmian mechanics

The ontology includes both ψ and one actual particle configuration X(t) in each run. For a spinless one-dimensional particle, the guiding law is dX/dt = j/ρ, where j=Im(ψ*∂xψ) in ℏ=m=1 units. The gold curves show a sampled equilibrium ensemble of configurations, obtained by the equivalent 1D cumulative-probability construction; they are not multiple particles in one run and not classical guesses.
